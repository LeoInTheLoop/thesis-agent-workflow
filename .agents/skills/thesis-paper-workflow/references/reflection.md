# Reflection Rules

Use this reference when drafting or editing the reflection document.

## Required Scope

Many institutions require a reflection chapter at the end of the thesis (often 800–1,000 words). Confirm the exact length and required questions from the official instruction PDF before drafting.

Typical questions a reflection chapter answers:

- How the thesis aligns with course / programme objectives.
- How planning worked and what could be improved.
- How the thesis relates to the broader education.
- Value for future work or studies.
- Satisfaction with execution and outcome.
- AI or similar tools used, how they were used, how well they worked, and concrete examples.

If the institution provides its own list of questions, follow that list exactly — do not substitute the generic list above.

## Preferred Structure

Use natural section headings rather than a question-answer form. Suggested headings:

- Course Objectives
- Planning
- Relation to My Education
- Future Value
- Satisfaction with the Outcome
- Use of AI Tools

## Voice

- Reflection may use **first person** (`I`, `my`).
- Keep the tone honest, specific, and grounded — not defensive, not a sales pitch.
- It is acceptable, and often expected, to state what went well **and** what was weaker.
- Avoid making it read like a checklist or a defense speech.
- Do not let reflection voice bleed into the main body chapters — see `paper-quality.md`.

## Content Discipline

Even in first person, keep the same evidence-led discipline as the main body:

- Be specific about what was accomplished, what was partial, and what was not attempted.
- Tie each "satisfaction" or "regret" statement to a concrete example.
- Acknowledge supervisor / advisor guidance where it shaped the work.
- Avoid sweeping personal-growth claims that the thesis does not actually evidence.

## AI-Use Consistency

The reflection's AI-disclosure paragraph must stay consistent with any AI-disclosure statement elsewhere in the thesis. Update both together — never one in isolation.

A safe template wording, to be adapted to your actual usage:

- **Tool**: the specific assistant(s) used.
- **Role**: a limited writing / programming / verification aid.
- **Uses**: e.g., improving English expression, checking structure, summarising papers, diagnosing code errors, supporting numerical consistency checking.
- **Boundary**: research ideas, experimental design, results, scientific claims, methodological choices, analyses, and conclusions remain the author's own.
- **Verification**: original papers checked, code run, output files inspected manually, writing personally revised.

If you change the listed tools or roles here, update the AI-disclosure statement in the main thesis at the same time.
